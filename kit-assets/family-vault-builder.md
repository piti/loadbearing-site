# Build Your Family Vault

A **family vault** is the same idea as a context vault, pointed at your life instead of your work: one place that holds the stable facts about your household — who's in it, what you eat, how you travel, what each kid has going on — so that every time you ask an AI to *plan the trip, sort out dinner, find a gift, help a kid prep for an exam*, it starts from your real context instead of a blank page and twenty follow-up questions.

You only have to tell it once that one person won't touch seafood, that the family likes to *plan* trips rather than wing them, that one kid is deep into music and the other into theater. Said once, written down, it pays off every time after.

**How to use this:** paste everything below the line into your AI assistant. It will interview you one section at a time and build the vault as it goes. Do it in one sitting or across a week — start with **Food** and **Travel** (the highest-traffic shelves) and let the rest fill in over time.

One thing this guide takes seriously that a work vault doesn't have to: **keep it walled off.** Your family vault should live in its own place, and the assistants doing your *work* should not be able to read it — and vice versa. A grocery list has no business in a client deck; a client's data has no business in your kid's exam prep. Separation isn't paranoia, it's just keeping your worlds from bleeding into each other.

## PASTE FROM HERE

> You are helping me build a **Family Vault** — a small, durable set of notes about my household that you (and other assistants) can read to give me genuinely contextual help. We're building it by interview. Follow these rules.
>
> **The shape.** Create this structure (folders + markdown files, or whatever note system I use). One concern per file; keep them short and navigable:
>
> ```
> family-vault/
>   people/        one page per family member
>   food/          meal rotation, preferences, no-go list, cooking constraints
>   travel/        standing preferences + recurring logistics + per-trip plans
>   household/     homes, pets, vehicles, key dates, time zones  (NO secrets)
>   education/     each kid's school/college context
>   interests/     hobbies, media, the family's threads
> ```
>
> **How to interview.** Go **one section at a time, a few questions per turn.** Ask, let me answer in plain language, write it to the right file, then move on. Don't dump all the questions at once. If I skip something, leave a `TODO` and move on — a half-filled vault is useful; an interrogation is not.
>
> ### 1. People (do this first — it's the reusable core)
> For each person: their name and role; the stable facts; what they're **into right now**; anything an assistant helping *them* should know (how they like things explained, sensitivities, a strong preference). For gifts: birthday and clothing/shoe sizes. Write one file per person.
>
> ### 2. Food & cooking ⭐ (highest-traffic shelf)
> - The **real rotation** — the ~10–15 meals actually cooked on repeat (not aspirational ones).
> - Per person: strong loves, hard **no**s, and any **allergies or dietary restrictions** (flag allergies clearly — this is a safety field).
> - Any **household rule** — e.g. an ingredient the whole house avoids at shared meals, and why.
> - Cooking reality: weeknight time budget, equipment, who cooks.
> - Occasions: the go-to when guests come; each kid's birthday-dinner request; holiday traditions.
>
> ### 3. Travel ⭐ (recurs on every trip)
> - **Pace:** planned-to-the-hour, loose-and-spontaneous, or planned-with-room-to-flex?
> - **Lodging:** hotel vs. rental; must-haves (kitchen, walkable, parking); rough budget band.
> - **Trip types** the family loves vs. avoids (cities / nature / beach / road trips / resorts vs. local-immersion).
> - **Constraints to always honor:** anyone's mobility, motion sickness, dietary needs on the road, height/legroom.
> - **Who travels together** — always everyone, or sometimes splits (one parent + one kid, couples trips)?
> - Recurring logistics worth writing once: a home base you return to, a transit pattern, a connectivity/SIM routine.
>
> ### 4. Household
> Homes/bases; **pets** (names, needs); vehicles (useful for road trips); recurring dates and traditions; time-zone gaps with relatives elsewhere. **Never** write account numbers, card details, passwords, or government-ID numbers — note that a thing exists, keep the actual secret out.
>
> ### 5. Education (per kid)
> Current school/grade or college; what's **in flight** (an upcoming exam, an application, a decision being weighed); how they best study or what's worked before.
>
> ### 6. Interests & family culture
> Per person hobbies; what the family watches/reads (so you can recommend well); languages spoken at home; weekend rhythms and traditions. Note the **threads** — the things several people share — because those are gold for gifts, outings, and projects.
>
> ---
>
> **Disciplines — follow these the whole way:**
> - **Don't invent.** If I didn't say it, don't write it as fact. Mark anything you inferred as `(inferred)`, and ask rather than guess. A confident wrong detail about my family is worse than a blank.
> - **No secrets, ever.** No passwords, account numbers, card details, or ID numbers in any file. If I mention one, flag it and leave it out.
> - **Go light on sensitive things.** Health and anything about the kids: capture only what serves a real, recurring need (an allergy that affects every meal, yes; a medical history, no). Minimal, and pointers over records.
> - **Keep it separate from my work.** This vault is private to my household. Don't pull my work/client data into it, and it shouldn't be readable by the assistants I use for work. If you're set up to enforce that boundary, do; if not, tell me how to keep these worlds apart.
> - **Start high-use, fill as we go.** Food and Travel first. Don't try to complete every shelf today — leave `TODO`s and we'll add to it naturally over time.
>
> When a section's done, give me a one-line summary of what you wrote and where, then move to the next.

## Keeping it fed (the part that makes it worth having)

A family vault is only as good as how current it is — and keeping it current should take a minute, not an afternoon:

- **After a trip or a season:** update standing preferences with anything you learned ("apartments beat hotels with the kids," "that pace was too packed").
- **When something changes in passing:** a new hobby, a grade, a pet, a dietary shift — drop the one line in. The habit of *"that's a keeper, into the vault"* is the whole game.
- **Once in a while:** a quick read-through to catch anything stale (a kid's "upcoming exam" that's now last year).

The payoff compounds: six months in, *"plan us a long weekend"* and *"what should I make this week"* and *"gift ideas for her birthday"* all start from a page of real context instead of an empty prompt.
